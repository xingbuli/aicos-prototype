# AI Chief of Staff Interview Summary: Alex Lopez
Interviewee: Alex Lopez, Digital Director
Topic: AI Chief of Staff (AICOS) product discovery
Executive Summary
Alex sees strong potential value in an AI Chief of Staff that helps leaders stay aligned across cross-functional teams by tracking objectives, surfacing action items, and highlighting priorities that need attention. His biggest need is not simply note-taking, but a system that understands goals, tracks execution status, and helps maintain visibility as priorities and stakeholders shift.
Key Needs
•	Objective and action-item tracking: AICOS should track performance objectives across teams and keep staff-meeting action items visible, including whether each item is in progress, completed, or blocked.
•	Weekly preparation: The tool should identify key meetings that require preparation, highlight where content needs to be generated, and help prioritize conversations that need the most attention.
•	Cross-functional alignment: As work becomes more cross-functional and objectives change frequently, AICOS could help leaders understand what each group is trying to accomplish and where priorities are shifting.
•	Stakeholder engagement: Digital needs a clearer view of how it is engaging with other teams and stakeholders; AICOS could help maintain continuity in those relationships and approaches.
Current Workflow
Alex’s team currently tracks priorities through a combination of OKRs, monthly review materials, PowerPoint working documents, and Jira boards. Monthly reviews cover team activities, top priorities, and one or two key deliverables per person or team. Jira is used more for execution management, with new items loaded, assigned, and moved through the board.
Opportunity for AICOS
AICOS could reduce administrative burden and improve leadership focus by creating a single source of visibility across goals, meetings, action items, and stakeholder engagement. Alex estimated that administrative work can take 8 to 16 hours per week, or more than 20% of a leader’s time. AICOS would be most valuable if it could connect planning and execution workflows, summarize what requires attention, and proactively flag risks, blockers, or follow-ups.
Considerations and Concerns
Privacy is a key consideration. Alex does not record his 1:1 meetings and prefers to collaborate online in shared documents. Any AICOS workflow for direct-report conversations would need to respect privacy preferences, avoid intrusive recording, and support lightweight collaboration in existing tools.
Product Implications
•	Prioritize integrations with existing planning and execution tools, including PowerPoint-based review materials, OKR processes, and Jira.
•	Design around leadership workflows such as weekly planning, monthly reviews, staff meetings, and stakeholder follow-ups.
•	Focus on surfacing status, blockers, and priority shifts rather than simply generating meeting notes.
•	Provide privacy-conscious support for 1:1s and sensitive conversations, potentially through shared-document collaboration rather than recording.
# AICOS Interview Summary: Claudia Nep
Interviewee: Claudia Nep, Sr. Director, Stakeholder Relations & Communications
Team context: Leads a 22-person team and spends roughly 20% or more of her time on coordination, scheduling, and administrative work.
Executive Takeaway
Claudia sees strong potential for AICOS to move beyond basic administrative automation and become a project execution partner. The highest-value use case is helping teams translate objectives and OKRs into detailed roadmaps, actionable tasks, follow-up meetings, reminders, and progress tracking.
Current Workflow
•	Claudia’s team uses Microsoft Loop as a central hub for team project management.
•	Loop has improved tracking of initiatives, action items, team meetings, and collaborative presentations.
•	Loop is not typically used for individual meetings with external teams.
•	AICOS could help keep Loop current with tasks, assignments, reminders, and external-facing updates.
Primary Pain Points
•	Coordination drag: Scheduling follow-ups and maintaining project momentum are recurring barriers to efficiency.
•	Project execution gaps: Projects can stall when goals are not broken into clear tasks, owners, timelines, and next steps.
AICOS Opportunity Areas
•	Project roadmapping: Generate detailed roadmaps from objectives and OKRs, then walk backward into actions required to achieve goals.
•	Execution orchestration: Create tasks, assign owners, schedule follow-ups, send reminders, and track progress against deliverables.
•	Administrative automation: Streamline repetitive processes so the team can focus on higher-value strategic work.
•	Knowledge readiness: Maintain accurate internal and external-facing project materials for broader communication.
Adoption Considerations
•	Must-have capabilities: Calendar and time management, more efficient meetings, deliverable tracking, and accurate project progress visibility.
•	Concerns: Potential human impact on existing roles and whether AI can provide accurate, relevant output without excessive human intervention.
•	Onboarding preference: A human account manager should support setup, training, and adoption to ensure the AI works effectively for the user.
Data Access and Privacy
Claudia is comfortable sharing work-related data, including emails and chats, to accelerate AICOS onboarding if the data is already company-owned and access improves the AI’s usefulness and effectiveness.
Killer Feature
The most compelling AICOS feature is the ability to create detailed project roadmaps that break objectives into actionable steps and help orchestrate execution. While administrative automation is useful, the greatest value comes from helping teams turn strategic goals into coordinated action.

# Alfredo Ordonez — AI Chief of Staff Interview Summary
Role: Vice President of International Business | Team: 7 direct reports
Current workload and delegation potential
Alfredo estimates that 15–20% of his time is spent on coordination and administrative work that could potentially be delegated to a Chief of Staff (CoS) or AI CoS. Within that, approximately 5% is dedicated to reviewing slide decks and presentation materials. He currently has an Executive Assistant for travel and meeting coordination, and some recurring low-value work, such as cost management, is already delegated to a back-office manager.
Team management and meeting cadence
He runs biweekly team meetings but finds 1:1 meetings more effective than larger group meetings. The ideal meeting length is 30–40 minutes. While he has encouraged the team to use Power BI for tracking activities and objectives, adoption has been limited because the team perceives it as additional work. As a result, actions can be missed, delays occur, and he must spend time following up.
Highest-value CoS responsibilities
•	Track team KPIs, objectives, budgets, and strategic priorities.
•	Maintain momentum on objectives by nudging team members for updates and follow-through.
•	Support strategic agenda-setting and prioritization.
•	Monitor news, regulatory developments, and the competitive landscape, especially given the loss of prior information subscriptions after the reorganization.
•	Help improve presentation quality and consistency, including slide-deck review and alignment with a unified corporate style.
Access, communication, and training
He does not see major issues with an AI CoS accessing email or calendar data, but highly confidential information, such as team compensation, should remain restricted. Preferred communication channels are text first, then voice, then video or avatar-based interaction. Estimated onboarding time is two months for a human CoS and one month for an AI CoS, assuming access to email and calendar is granted.
Key concern
His primary concern is visibility and context. If an AI CoS tracks all activity, it could generate reports that appear to show inefficiencies without understanding the full context. For example, an in-person interview may not appear in the system if the AI CoS was not present, making it seem as though the meeting did not happen. The AI CoS must therefore be designed to recognize uncertainty, identify gaps in its knowledge, and avoid over-reporting conclusions without context.
